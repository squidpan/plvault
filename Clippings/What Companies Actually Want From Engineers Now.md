## The job description is lying to you

[***Non-member, click here to read***](https://medium.com/@sauravsinghsisodiya/what-companies-actually-want-from-engineers-now-d2b347f5ef95?sk=a2acf9e6e6405a1d5be84740dc3f07a5)

![](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:1400/format:webp/0*CY8iH_M2vdc5XdDL)

Photo by Mina Rad on Unsplash

When you open a job post, it feels like someone spun a wheel of buzzwords and pasted the result online.

Seven years of experience in a framework that barely existed four years ago.

“Rockstar engineer.”

“Self-starter.”

A giant wall of requirements where half the bullets don’t even relate to the actual work.

And somewhere at the bottom, almost hidden:

*“Strong communication skills are a plus.”*

Most job descriptions are not written by the people you’ll actually work with.

They’re recycled from an older hiring post, edited by HR, rushed through a manager’s inbox, then published because someone needed the role open by Friday.

They describe what the company *thought* it needed at some point. Not necessarily what matters today.

So if you’ve been trying to perfectly match every requirement and constantly feeling behind, part of the problem is the list itself.

Because the real hiring criteria changed quietly over the last few years.

And a lot of people still haven’t noticed.

## The bar shifted without announcing itself

A while ago, being technically strong was enough.

If you knew your stack, wrote clean code, fixed bugs quickly, and generally delivered work on time, you were already ahead of most people.

That still matters.

Obviously.

But by itself, it doesn’t carry the same weight anymore.

The uncomfortable reality is that AI can already generate decent chunks of working code. Not amazing code. Not thoughtful architecture. But enough to handle a surprising amount of implementation work.

Things junior engineers used to spend two days figuring out can now appear in thirty seconds.

Teams know this. Even the ones pretending they don’t.

So the value naturally moves upward.

The question companies ask now isn’t just:

“Can this person code?”

It’s closer to:

“Can this person understand what’s worth building in the first place?”

And honestly, the engineers getting the biggest opportunities right now are often not the most brilliant programmers in the room.

They’re the people who behave like they own outcomes instead of tasks.

That difference matters more than most resumes show.

## What companies actually care about now

## Ownership. The real version, not the LinkedIn version.

Every engineering manager remembers *that* person.

The engineer who notices production behaving weirdly before alerting even to fire.

The one already replying in Slack when something breaks late at night.

The one who fixes obvious problems without waiting for permission or a Jira ticket.

Those people are rare.

A lot of engineers are good at completing assigned work. Fewer actually care whether the system succeeds after deployment.

There’s a difference between:

- “The PR merged.”
- “The feature shipped.”
- “The problem got solved.”

The last one is what companies actually remember.

Ownership shows up in tiny moments.

Do you follow up without reminders?

Do you write incident notes after something fails?

Do you raise risks early instead of surprising everyone two days before launch?

Managers notice this stuff more than people think.

Especially during stressful periods.

And when layoffs, budget cuts, or reorganizations happen, the people trusted with ownership tend to survive them.

That sounds harsh, but it’s true.

## Engineers who can talk to non-engineers

I know.

Most engineers hate hearing this.

But this isn’t about becoming some polished corporate speaker.

It’s about being able to explain technical tradeoffs like a normal human being.

Because engineering decisions don’t stay inside engineering teams anymore.

Product managers want context.

Sales teams want estimates.

Leadership wants to understand risk.

Customers ask technical questions directly.

And the engineers who can explain complicated things without sounding defensive or overly technical become incredibly valuable.

Imagine a PM says:

“Can we add Google login before launch?”

A weak answer is:

“Yes.”

Or:

“No.”

A useful answer sounds more like:

“We can, but here’s what it affects, here’s the risk if we rush it, and here’s the safer option if timing matters.”

That kind of communication changes careers.

Seriously.

Once people trust you to explain tradeoffs clearly, they pull you into bigger conversations.

And once you’re in those rooms, your influence grows fast.

## Adaptability matters more than stack loyalty now

A lot of engineers still build their entire identity around one technology.

That used to work better.

Now it’s risky.

Not because your favorite stack is disappearing tomorrow, but because teams constantly shift priorities now.

The engineering companies that are valued most are usually the ones that can move with those shifts instead of resisting them.

Real adaptability is not listing ten languages on a resume.

It’s being able to enter an unfamiliar codebase on Monday and contribute meaningful work by Thursday.

It’s learning by doing instead of endlessly preparing.

It’s saying:

“I haven’t worked with this exact tool before, but I can figure it out.”

And actually proving it.

That signal matters more than certifications most of the time.

Companies care less about how many tools you know and more about whether you’ve successfully learned difficult things before.

## Knowing when not to build something

This one barely gets discussed, but experienced teams care about it a lot.

An unbelievable amount of engineering time gets wasted building things nobody truly needed.

Features customers barely use.

Massive rewrites that change almost nothing.

Integrations were added because a competitor had them.

The engineers who ask:

“Wait… what problem are we solving here?”

Before writing code are incredibly valuable.

Not because they block progress.

Because they prevent expensive distractions.

Sometimes one uncomfortable question saves months of work.

And companies eventually learn which engineers consistently bring that kind of judgment into planning conversations.

## Engineers who actually know how to use AI

Not casually.

Not “I tried ChatGPT once.”

Actually use it well.

There’s already a noticeable gap forming between engineers who deeply integrate AI into their workflow and those who don’t.

The productivity difference is honestly kind of wild sometimes.

Good engineers use AI for:

- debugging
- exploring approaches
- generating boilerplate
- pressure-testing ideas
- summarizing unfamiliar systems
- accelerating repetitive work

But the important part is judgment.

Strong engineers still verify everything.

They know when the output is wrong.

They know when *not* to trust it.

That combination is becoming extremely valuable.

And yes, companies are starting to quietly evaluate this during interviews now.

## What’s losing value

Some skills that used to stand out simply don’t carry the same advantage anymore.

## Memorizing everything

Being the person who remembers every API method or obscure syntax detail used to look impressive.

Now?

Most people can retrieve that information instantly.

Knowledge still matters.

Memorization matters less.

## Extreme specialization with zero flexibility

Companies still need experts.

But they increasingly avoid engineers who only function inside one tiny technical bubble.

Especially if collaboration becomes difficult.

Teams want depth.

But they also want engineers who can operate outside narrow boundaries when needed.

## The lone wolf engineer

The old story used to sound heroic:

An engineer disappears for weeks and returns with a giant system nobody else understands.

Now that mostly scares companies.

Because systems that only one person understands become operational risks.

A lot of organizations learned that lesson the hard way.

## Interviews are changing too

Slowly.

Unevenly.

But definitely changing.

Algorithm-heavy interviews still exist, especially at large tech companies.

But many teams now focus more on judgment and decision-making.

You’ll see more conversations like:

“Tell me about a technical disagreement.”

“What failed in your last project?”

“What would you do differently now?”

Those questions reveal way more than memorized LeetCode solutions.

Interviewers increasingly look for engineers who understand systems, tradeoffs, communication, prioritization, and business impact.

One thing interviewers quietly notice fast:

Engineers who only talk about code.

Strong candidates usually explain:

- Why were decisions made
- What constraints existed
- What tradeoffs mattered
- what they learned afterward

That framing changes how people perceive your seniority almost immediately.

## If you want to shift how companies see you

A few things genuinely help.

Not productivity-hack nonsense. Actual useful things.

## Write about decisions, not tutorials

The internet already has enough “How to install X” articles.

What stands out more is writing about:

- architectural tradeoffs
- failed decisions
- scaling problems
- lessons learned
- Why your team chose one approach over another

That kind of writing signals judgment.

And judgment is increasingly what companies hire for.

## Join more cross-functional discussions

Volunteer for planning meetings occasionally.

Listen to customer calls.

Understand how the business actually works.

A surprising number of engineers stay isolated from the business context for years.

The ones who don’t usually grow faster.

## Build things people can point to

Side projects still matter.

Internal tools matter.

Open source contributions matter.

Shipped work creates credibility over time in a way certifications rarely do.

Even small projects compound.

## Learn AI tools properly

Not casually.

Spend real time understanding where they help and where they completely fail.

The engineers getting the biggest leverage from AI right now are not blindly accepting outputs.

They’re using AI as amplification, not replacement.

That distinction matters a lot.

## Where is this heading

Engineering is not disappearing.

If anything, demand for people who can build useful systems is still massive.

But the definition of a valuable engineer is evolving.

Technical skill is still the foundation.

That part hasn’t changed.

What changed is everything layered on top of it:

- judgment
- communication
- adaptability
- ownership
- business awareness
- decision-making

The gap between “good coder” and “engineer people trust with important problems” has become much larger.

And honestly, most of that gap has very little to do with syntax or frameworks.

The good part is that these skills are learnable.

Nobody naturally starts with all of them.

But the engineers who deliberately build them now are probably going to have a very unfair advantage over the next few years.
