## Nobody cared about his side project either. He built it in 2 months, got calls from Sam Altman and Zuckerberg, and the offers hit billions.

Your day job is killing your edge.

You ship the same CRUD endpoints and build internal tools. You sit in the same standups. You wait for permission to try anything new.

Meanwhile, the industry shifts under your feet every six months. Side projects keep you sharp, keep you employable. And might build something that changes your life.

This is my push to you. Start tonight.

## Your Day Job Is Not Enough

Most jobs use a tiny slice of your skills. You touch one framework. You solve one type of problem. You become a specialist in your company’s tech stack.

That stack may not exist in five years.

Side projects pull you out of that cage. You pick the tools. You own the architecture. You make every decision that someone else makes for you at work.

That freedom builds real skill. I learned this the hard way.

## You Learn Faster Than Any Course

Tutorials lie to you. They show clean code and finished apps. They skip the messy debugging part that makes you a real engineer.

Side projects teach you what tutorials cannot.

You hit weird bugs. You debug your own database choices. You learn why senior engineers care about boring things. Logging and error handling.

One side project beats ten courses. Every time.

## You Stay Ahead of the Curve

AI changes everything every quarter. New frameworks drop every Tuesday. Tools you trusted last year feel ancient now.

Reading blog posts will not save you.

You need hands on the keyboard. You need to break things with the new tools. You need to understand why one approach wins over another.

Side projects keep you current. Engineers without them fall behind in silence.

## The Money Argument

==Some side projects turn into real money. A small SaaS. A Chrome extension. A digital product. A weekend tool that solves one painful problem.==

Even small projects can pull in steady monthly income.

That is rent in some cities. That is your kid’s school fees. That is freedom from one toxic boss.

You will not know which project becomes the money maker. You only find out by shipping.

## Real Developers, Real Side Projects

Forget the cliche startup stories for a minute. Here are recent stories of devs who built something on the side and changed their lives.

## Peter Steinberger’s OpenClaw

In November 2025, Peter Steinberger spent about an hour wiring two pieces of software together.

He wanted an AI assistant he could text over WhatsApp to find restaurants in areas.

He called it a playground project.

The repo, eventually renamed OpenClaw, went from zero to 346,000 GitHub stars in under five months. Sam Altman messaged him. Mark Zuckerberg reached out via WhatsApp. Both made offers in the billions.

In February 2026, Steinberger joined OpenAI to lead their work on personal agents.

He has said publicly that OpenClaw was project number 44 for him. Forty-three things nobody cared about. Then one that worked.

## Josh Wardle’s Wordle

Regular software engineer. Worked at Reddit. Back in 2013, he hacked together a tiny word game prototype. Then shelved it and forgot about it for years.

During COVID, he picked it up again as a gift for his partner. She loved word games. He wanted something they could play together each day. Just the two of them, while the world stayed locked inside.

They played in secret for months before he showed it to his family. Then friends. In October 2021, he put it on his personal site, no marketing, no ads, no plan.

Ninety players on November 1st. Three hundred thousand by January. Two million the week after that.

In January 2022, the New York Times bought Wordle for over a million dollars. Wardle never wanted to monetize it. He built a love letter for one person, and the whole world picked it up off the floor.

## Markus Persson’s Minecraft

Just a regular game programmer at Midasplayer. The company that later rebranded to King and made Candy Crush. He spent his days on browser games in Flash and felt completely boxed in.

So he hacked on weird hobby projects after working hours.

In May 2009, he posted a rough block-building game on an indie forum called TIGSource. He charged a small fee for early access while he kept building it.

People started buying. Then more people. Then a lot more.

That hobby project was Minecraft.

Microsoft later bought it for $2.5 billion. Almost half of them went directly into Markus’s pocket.

All from a random weekend hobby on some indie forum.

## What These Stories Share

None of these devs set out to build something huge.

Steinberger called his project a playground. Wardle made a game for his girlfriend. Notch hacked on weekends to break free from his day job.

They all scratched their own itch. They all shipped before they felt ready. They all kept building when nobody cared yet. And the world found them, not the other way around.

Your project might do nothing. Or it might be the one that changes everything. You will not know until you ship.

## Stop Worrying About Users

Here is the part that matters most. Your first ten projects will get zero users. Your next ten might get five users each.

That is fine.

Forget viral success. Aim to get better. Each project teaches you what really matters about shipping real things.

> ***You are training. Athletes do not skip practice because nobody watches.***

## Build Scrap and Move On

Most projects deserve to die. You build for two weeks. You realize the idea is weak. You move on.

That is not failure. That is good filtering.

The worst thing you can do is force a bad idea for six months. Kill it fast. Start the next one. My tenth idea was better than my first by a mile.

Scrap is the price of finding gold.

## Your Portfolio Becomes Your Resume

Recruiters skim resumes for six seconds. A live project stops them cold.

A real link beats a buzzword list.

Hiring managers love builders. They can spot real experience quickly. Studying React is one thing. Shipping an app for real users is another. Side projects prove you can finish things.

That signal is rare and valuable.

## You Build a Network Without Trying

Ship a side project and post about it. People with the same problem find you. Other builders reach out. Some of them become friends, partners, or future cofounders.

This network does not exist for resume holders.

It exists for builders. The internet rewards people who make things in public.

## How to Start Tonight

Pick one annoying problem in your own life. Not a big idea. Not a unicorn. Just one small thing that bugs you.

Build the smallest version that works.

Ship it before the weekend ends. Tell three people. Start the next one. Repeat it for a year. Your career will not look the same.

> Keep building. That is the only rule.

Most developers quit after their first project flops. The ones who win keep shipping through the silence. They trust the process when nothing works yet.

Your tenth project is closer than you think.

So close your tabs. Open your editor. Build the dumb thing tonight.

In the future, you will thank yourself for starting.
