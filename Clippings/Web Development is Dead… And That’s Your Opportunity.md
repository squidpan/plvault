## The market is flooded with developers. Mostly fake ones.

> [Click here to read for free](https://medium.com/@brevis08/web-development-is-dead-and-thats-your-opportunity-1d4063991897?sk=453803346c95c864adf69413602867bc)

Everyone thinks web development is oversaturated now.

They’re right. It is.

There are millions of people learning React, building the same projects, applying to the same junior jobs, and wondering why nobody replies to their applications anymore.

And honestly, a lot of them should probably stop.

Because the market is not saturated with developers.

It’s saturated with people who can follow tutorials.

Those are not the same thing.

Every bootcamp, YouTube channel, and “roadmap” is creating the exact same developer.

Same portfolio. Same weather app. Same to do app. Same fake full stack SaaS dashboard with authentication copied from a tutorial.

Then people act shocked when companies don’t care.

The barrier to entry became so low that the average applicant is basically interchangeable.

And weirdly, that’s the opportunity.

If 95% of people are doing the same thing, being even slightly different suddenly becomes valuable.

Not genius level different. Just real.

## The problem nobody wants to admit

A lot of people getting into web development are not trying to become good at building software.

They’re trying to escape into a “tech job”.

That’s why tutorial hell exists.

Because tutorials feel productive. You finish a course and get a dopamine hit without ever making decisions yourself.

But real development is mostly decisions.

You have vague requirements.  
Things break randomly.  
Dependencies fight each other.  
Your architecture turns into garbage after six months.  
You debug issues that make no sense.

That’s the actual job.

And most people never practice that part.

They only practice repetition.

## Companies don’t actually want “React developers”

This is the funny part.

People read job titles too literally.

The posting says “Frontend Developer” or “React Developer”, so everyone rushes to memorize hooks and build another clone project.

But companies are usually looking for something else entirely.

They want someone who can think through messy problems without needing a step by step video every 10 minutes.

Someone who can take incomplete requirements and still build something usable.

Someone who understands trade offs.

Frameworks matter, sure. But frameworks are replaceable every few years anyway. The people who survive long term usually understand fundamentals underneath the framework hype cycle.

Most developers skip that part because fundamentals are slower and less exciting.

Nobody makes a YouTube thumbnail saying

> “Understanding closures changed my life.”

## Entry level is crowded because everybody looks identical

People keep saying web development is dead because junior hiring got worse.

That’s true to some extent.

But look at what happened.

Thousands of people entered the industry through the exact same learning pipeline at the exact same time.

Bootcamp → React → portfolio → apply everywhere.

Of course companies became numb to it.

If you review 500 portfolios and 470 of them look identical, you stop caring about portfolios entirely.

This is why “build projects” became bad advice.

Most people interpreted that advice in the laziest possible way.

The issue was never projects themselves. The issue was building tutorial projects with zero real-world constraints.

There’s a massive difference between

> “I built a Netflix clone from YouTube”

and

> “I built a scheduling system for a local business and had to deal with real users complaining when it broke.”

One of those teaches development.

The other teaches imitation.

## Most developers are too broad and too shallow

Another unpopular thing:

==“Full stack developer” has become almost meaningless at junior level.==

A lot of people know a tiny bit of everything and not enough of anything.

Sometimes being more specific makes you more employable.

Accessibility.  
Performance.  
Security.  
Complex state management.  
Developer tooling.  
Testing infrastructure.

These things are boring until a company desperately needs somebody who actually understands them.

Then suddenly that skill becomes valuable very fast.

Meanwhile everybody else is arguing about which JavaScript framework feels more “modern” this month.

## The market probably gets harder from here

AI tools will absolutely remove some low-level work.

Companies are also getting more selective because they can.

And there are still too many people entering web development expecting fast money after finishing a course.

So yes, the easy era is probably over.

But honestly, that might be healthy.

Because the people who survive tougher markets usually end up much better.

Not because they’re smarter.

Because they were forced to actually learn how things work.

And maybe that’s the weird part nobody talks about.

Web development becoming harder might be the best thing that happened to people who genuinely care about building things.
